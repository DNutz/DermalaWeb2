What Is the Microbiome?

The microbiome refers to all the living organisms present on your skin, which includes thousands of different types of bacteria. Many of these bacteria are necessary to the health of our skin as they can not only help prevent infections, but also promote radiant skin.

Why is it so important?

Many beauty products on the market are actually harming our microbiome and the health of our skin. Our skin is under constant assault from an overuse of antibiotics and other cosmetic products; even soap can be harmful to our microbiome, killing off a lot of our good bacteria.

What can we do?

Save your skin and switch to natural and biome-friendly products. Only a few companies like Dermala strive to protect your microbiome with skincare and acne products that only contain natural ingredients, free from harmful agents.

Overview of Acne Pathogenesis

Acne occurs when glands in the follicles of the skin become overactive, producing more oil than needed. This oil along with dead skin cells can form a plug that blocks the follicle. Bacteria that live on the skin – and which are usually completely harmless – can then infect the plugged follicles, causing acne.

Types of Acne

  • Blackheads: Blackheads occur when a pore is clogged by a combination of sebum and dead skin cells. The top of the pore stays open, despite the rest of it being clogged. This results in the characteristic black color seen on the surface.
  • Whiteheads: Whiteheads can also form when a pore gets clogged by sebum and dead skin cells. But unlike with blackheads, the top of the pore closes up. It looks like a small bump protruding from the skin.
  • Papules: Papules occur when the walls surrounding your pores break down from severe inflammation. This results in hard, clogged pores that are tender to the touch. The skin around these pores is usually pink.
  • Pustules: Pustules can also form when the walls around your pores break down. Unlike papules, pustules are filled with pus. These bumps come out from the skin and are usually red in color. They often have yellow or white heads on top.

Association between Acne and P. Acnes Overgrowth

P. acnes bacteria live deep within follicles and pores, away from the surface of the skin. Elevated production of oil or blockage of the follicle can cause P. acnes bacteria to grow and multiply. P. acnes bacteria secrete many proteins, including several digestive enzymes, which can destabilize the walls of the follicle. The cellular damage, metabolic byproducts and bacterial debris produced by the rapid growth of P. acnes in follicles can trigger inflammation, which can lead to the symptoms associated with acne.

How Acne is Typically Treated

  • Benzoyl peroxide: It is an anti-bacterial that kills the P. Acnes bacteria that contribute to acne.
  • Salicylic acid: It is an exfoliant that can effectively penetrate and get rid of the material clogging the pore.
  • Note: These two are often used together because the salicylic acid clears the way for the benzoyl peroxide to get into the pore and kill the bacteria.
  • Retinoids: Retinoids are naturally occurring forms of Vitamin A. They fight breakouts by normalizing the shedding of dead skin cells that clog pores.
  • Antibiotics: These are used for moderate to severe acne and are only available through a physician. Due to progressively worsening antibiotic resistance, antibiotics become less and less effective as acne treatments over time.

Microbiome-based acne treatment options

Most acne treatments attack our microbiomes in order to get rid of the bad bacteria that cause acne. Microbiome-based acne treatments aim to target only the bad bacteria and work with the good bacteria in order to maintain a healthy and balanced microbiome. By doing this, these treatments not only help reduce acne but also avoid nasty side effects such as itchy, red, sensitive or dry skin.
